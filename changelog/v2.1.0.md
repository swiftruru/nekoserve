# NekoServe v2.1.0 -- Validation page auditability: methodology citations, per-metric breakdowns, benchmark provenance, and teachable formulas

Released: 2026-04-23

v2.1.0 transforms the Validation page from a black-box score into an auditable research tool. Every number on the page can now be traced back to its source: the framework it comes from (Sargent 2013, Kleijnen 1995, Rubner 2000), the formula that produced it (with clickable-symbol chip explanations), the paper's Figure/Table the benchmark value was extracted from, and the 95% Wilson confidence interval the simulation is being compared against. The statistical math itself (χ² / KS / KL composite scoring) is unchanged — v2.1 is purely an auditability, documentation, and teaching layer on top of the existing validator.

## The driving problem

Before v2.1, the Validation page showed three sub-scores (χ² = 93, KS = 74, KL = 93) and a composite 87/100, but offered no way to answer "where does 87 come from?" The methodology wasn't cited, the formulas were hidden, the Hirsch 2025 reference values were bare numbers with no provenance, and single-number sub-scores gave no hint about which category drove the deviation. For a thesis defence, this reads as "the author says the score is 87", which is the opposite of scientific transparency.

v2.1 makes every step of the scoring pipeline user-accessible. Examiners can now click any score or benchmark number and see its formula, its source paper, its statistical interpretation, and its per-category contribution.

## A. Methodology section (Verification & Validation framework)

New collapsible "🎓 Validation methodology" card at the top of the Validation page, structured in three sub-blocks:

**A.1 V&V framework citations** — three new canonical citations back the multi-metric composite approach:

- **Sargent (2013)** *Verification and validation of simulation models* (Winter Simulation Conference, 6000+ citations) — backs the multi-indicator composite design
- **Kleijnen (1995)** *Verification and validation of simulation models* (Eur. J. Oper. Res.) — backs χ² goodness-of-fit as the default for categorical distribution comparison
- **Rubner et al. (2000)** *The Earth Mover's Distance as a Metric for Image Retrieval* (IJCV) — backs why complementary metrics, not a single one

A prominent amber callout surfaces the critical methodological alignment: **Hirsch 2025 itself uses χ² goodness-of-fit to validate its observations** (χ²(2) = 1234.2, χ²(4) = 102.61, χ²(4) = 82.386, χ²(16) = 1113.2, all p < 0.001 with Bonferroni correction). Our validation method is the same method the paper uses — not a new invention, not an arbitrary choice.

**A.2 Indicator complementarity matrix** — a 3×3 table documenting what each metric detects, what its blind spot is, and which metric covers it:

| Indicator | Detects | Blind spot | Paired with |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| χ² chi-square | One category over/under-weighted | Distribution shape shifts | KS |
| KS max gap | Where in the CDF the largest deviation sits | Small categories drowned out | χ² |
| KL divergence | Probability mass on wrong categories (information loss) | Asymmetric, direction-dependent | χ² |

**A.3 Threshold justification** — explains where the soft-cap constants and the 80-point pass threshold come from:

- χ² soft-cap 0.5 is deliberately ~30× looser than Agresti (2013)'s hard critical value 15.51 at df = 8, α = 0.05, because we're in proportion space (not count space) and need headroom for Monte Carlo noise
- KS soft-cap 0.3 is ~20× looser than Massey (1951)'s D₀.₀₅ ≈ 1.36/√n = 0.0147 for n = 8,547, same noise-tolerance rationale
- 80-point pass threshold follows Sargent (2013)'s recommended face-validity ≥ 0.8 convention
- Explicit caveat that soft-caps are engineering heuristics, not rigorous hypothesis tests — raw p-values are surfaced separately per metric

Every prose sentence in this section runs through `renderWithTerms`, so V&V-specific jargon (goodness-of-fit, face validity, outlier, Type I error, multi-metric composite, soft-cap, V&V, etc.) gets an inline glossary tooltip on hover.

## B. Per-metric breakdown panels

Each of the three sub-score cards (χ² / KS / KL) is now a clickable button that expands a full teaching-grade breakdown below the score grid. One metric can be expanded at a time; clicking the same card collapses it, clicking another swaps.

**B.1 ChiSquareBreakdown** — 9-row residual contribution table showing every category's observed O, expected E, raw residual (O − E), standardised residual (O − E)/√E, raw contribution (O − E)²/E, and each row's share of the total χ². The "share of total" column is rendered as a horizontal bar chart so the dominant contributor (e.g. "SHELF drives 40% of χ²") is visually obvious. Standardised residuals with |value| ≥ 2 render in bold red as attention flags. Bottom row sums to the published χ² value. Below the table, an interpretation callout cites Agresti 2013's χ² table for df = k − 1 and notes which p-value bucket the statistic falls into.

**B.2 KSCumulativePlot** — 600×200 SVG rendering both CDFs (empirical in amber, simulation in orange) over the 9 categories. A red vertical line marks the exact category where the maximum absolute gap sits, with the D value labelled in-line. The matching table below highlights the peak-gap row in red and lists each category's running cumulative O, cumulative E, and |O − E|. The interpretation callout cites Massey's 1951 critical value D₀.₀₅ = 1.36/√n and honestly notes the tension between "strict KS says reject at this n" and the soft-cap design choice that still maps the statistic to a passing 74 points — showing the reader the tradeoff instead of hiding it.

**B.3 KLContributionBars** — per-category p·log(p/q) divergent bar chart. The center axis separates positive contributions (simulation over-commits, rendered orange) from negative contributions (simulation under-commits, rendered sky blue). Each row shows p = simulation share, q = empirical share, the signed contribution in nats, and a visual bar extending left or right from the midline. Total at the bottom matches the published KL. Interpretation callout reports `exp(−KL) × 100%` as the approximation fidelity reading (KL = 0.020 → ~98.1% fidelity ≈ 2% information loss).

## C. Benchmark provenance audit trail

New collapsible "📜 Benchmark provenance" card beneath the benchmark header. Documents every single number in `benchmarks.ts` with its source paper location, raw observation count, and 95% Wilson score confidence interval.

### Data structure change

`benchmarks.ts` switches from `Record<string, number>` to:

```typescript
interface CategoryBenchmark {
  proportion: number                      // what we had before
  n: number                               // observation count
  total: number                           // sample total
  wilsonCI95: [number, number]            // derived from (n, total)
  source: {
    figureOrTable: string                 // e.g. "Figure 3, Results §3.2"
    page?: number
    note?: string                         // e.g. "n estimated from % × 12,505 total scans"
  }
}
```

`validator.ts` adapts with a single `proportionMap(record)` helper — the statistical calculations themselves are unchanged.

### What the card shows

Two tables (Figure 3 behavior distribution + Figure 3 right-panel vertical level), each with:

- Category (e.g. SHELF)
- Empirical %
- n (raw observation count)
- 95% Wilson CI (e.g. [48.2%, 50.3%])
- Simulation %
- ✓ or ⚠ whether the simulation value falls inside the CI
- Source (e.g. "Figure 3 right panel — n reported directly: 1560 (floor) + 2778 (furniture) + 4209 (shelf) = 8547")

### Hirsch 2025 PDF values now grounded

Numbers extracted directly from the PDF and surfaced via the audit trail:

- Total scans: **12,505** across 107 observations, 70 days, 27 cats, 227 hours (Figure 3 caption)
- Vertical in-lounge subtotal: **n = 8547** = 1560 floor + 2778 furniture + 4209 shelf (reported directly)
- Behavior 9-category n values estimated from reported % × 12,505, flagged in the `note` field

### Paper's own statistical tests surfaced

The card lists the four χ² tests Hirsch 2025 itself runs on this data:

- χ²(2) = 1234.2, p < 0.001 (vertical level goodness-of-fit)
- χ²(4) = 102.61, p < 0.001 (vertical × stay period)
- χ²(4) = 82.386, p < 0.001 (vertical × customer occupancy)
- χ²(16) = 1113.2, p < 0.001 (behavior × stay period)

All with Bonferroni-corrected padj = 0.013 (for vertical) or padj < 0.025 (for behavior). This is the empirical anchor for the "we use the same method as the paper" claim in section A.

### Wilson score CI (Wilson 1927)

New [`validation/wilsonCI.ts`](src/renderer/src/validation/wilsonCI.ts) computes the confidence interval via:

```
CI_95% = (p̂ + z²/(2n) ± z·√(p̂(1−p̂)/n + z²/(4n²))) / (1 + z²/n)
```

Preferred over the Wald interval p̂ ± 1.96·√(p̂(1−p̂)/n) because Wald collapses to zero width at p̂ near 0 or 1 and is too narrow at small n (Agresti & Coull 1998). The formula displays as a clickable teaching card (see section E).

## D. Jargon glossary expansion (19 new bilingual terms)

The shared `TermTooltip` glossary — already covering DES/queueing terms used elsewhere in the app — picks up 19 new validation-specific terms. Any prose mentioning these now auto-wraps with a hover popover showing the term's label and definition:

`goodness-of-fit`, `Kolmogorov-Smirnov` (KS), `Kullback-Leibler divergence` (KL), `Wilson score`, `CDF`, `p-value`, `critical value`, `degrees of freedom`, `standardized residual`, `residual`, `nats`, `Monte Carlo`, `Bonferroni correction`, `face validity`, `multi-metric composite`, `soft-cap`, `Type I error`, `outlier`, `Verification & Validation` (V&V)

All definitions are bilingual (Traditional Chinese + English) and written to be approachable for students who are not yet statistically fluent.

## E. Teachable formulas: clickable symbol chips

All six formulas on the Validation page now render via `InteractiveFormula` — the clickable-chip teaching widget already used on the Results and Settings pages. Students can click any symbol to expand a card showing:

- `label` — what this symbol is called
- `desc` — 2-3 sentence plain-language description
- `unit` — what units, if any (e.g. "0 ~ 1", "nats", "dimensionless")
- `example` — a concrete example plugging in this simulation's actual numbers

### Six teachable formulas

1. **Composite score**: `score = 0.4·χ²_norm + 0.3·KS_norm + 0.3·KL_norm` — 6 chips (score, each sub-score, each weight)
2. **Soft-cap mapping**: `score = 100·max(0, 1 − stat/cap)` — 4 chips (stat, cap, max guard, percent scale)
3. **Pearson χ²**: `χ² = Σᵢ (Oᵢ − Eᵢ)²/Eᵢ` — 6 chips (χ², Oᵢ, Eᵢ, squared residual, Σ, k)
4. **KS D statistic**: `D = max_x |F_sim(x) − F_emp(x)|` — 5 chips (D, F_sim, F_emp, max, absolute value)
5. **KL divergence**: `D_KL(P‖Q) = Σ pᵢ log(pᵢ/qᵢ)` — 6 chips (D_KL, P, Q, pᵢ, qᵢ, log-ratio)
6. **Wilson 95% CI**: full score-test expression — 5 chips (p̂, n, z, bias term, ±)

Each formula also carries a persistent 🔍 one-line plain-language hint below the chips, and an optional "更多說明 ▼" expander for deep-dive prose (asymmetry considerations, alternative metrics, derivation shortcuts).

One shared [`FormulaBox`](src/renderer/src/components/validation/FormulaBox.tsx) wrapper applies a consistent orange-bar callout style and the expand/collapse state management. Pattern reused for the single Poisson-style teaching formula already shipped elsewhere.

## Voice

The methodology section and interpretation callouts follow a mixed voice: formal when citing external papers ("Sargent 2013 recommends multi-indicator composites"), first-person when explaining design choices ("I picked three complementary indicators because…"). This matches the existing app copy style — readable, opinionated, traceable — rather than sliding into third-person academic detachment.

## Author's note

This release is a direct response to advisor feedback: "your 87/100 looks like a black box — where does it come from?" The math was already sound; the problem was that the page didn't *teach* the math. v2.1 doesn't add a new statistical technique. It makes the existing one defensible in front of a thesis committee by putting every step behind a clickable explanation. If someone asks "why 80?", "why χ²?", "where does 49.2% come from?", or "how did you compute the CI?" — the answer is now one click away, on the same page, with the citation attached.
